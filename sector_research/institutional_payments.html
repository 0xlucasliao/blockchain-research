I
Foundation

The Ecosystem

B2B and institutional payments operate across two interconnected layers — an institutional/interbank layer governed by central banks and clearinghouses, and a corporate layer where buyers, suppliers, and service providers transact.

Unlike consumer payments, B2B transactions are characterised by high value, multi-party approval chains, rich remittance data requirements, and complex compliance obligations. A single payment instruction may touch six or more entities before funds reach their destination.

Layer I Institutional & Interbank Layer
Central Banks

Operate RTGS systems for final settlement. Provide liquidity to commercial banks. Set monetary policy and reserve requirements.

Settlement authority
Clearinghouses

Net and batch payment obligations between member banks. Key institutions: CHIPS (US), EBA Clearing (EU), HKICL (HK), CIPS (China).

Netting & clearing
SWIFT Network

Global messaging cooperative connecting 11,000+ institutions. Migrating from legacy MT formats to ISO 20022 (MX) for richer data carriage.

Messaging infrastructure
Correspondent Banks

Maintain nostro/vostro accounts to facilitate cross-border flows. Form the chain through which international wires are routed and de-risked.

Cross-border routing
Custodians & CSDs

Hold securities and facilitate delivery-versus-payment (DvP) for institutional trades. Link financial markets to the payment system.

Securities settlement
Regulatory Bodies

BIS, FATF, FSB, national regulators — set capital, AML, and operational resilience standards that govern every participant in the chain.

Oversight
Layer II Corporate B2B Layer
Buyer / Payer

Initiates payment via ERP or treasury management system (TMS). Manages spend controls, approval hierarchies, and cash forecasting.

AP function
Payment Service Providers

Banks, fintech platforms, and payment networks that process, route, and settle corporate payments. Often provide FX, fraud, and data services.

Processing
Supplier / Payee

Receives funds into their AR system. Matches incoming payments to open invoices. Reconciliation quality depends on remittance data richness.

AR function
Compliance & KYB

Know-Your-Business screening, AML monitoring, OFAC/EU sanctions checking, and PEP screening — often duplicated across each institution in the chain.

Risk & compliance
FX & Treasury

Cross-currency conversion, forward hedging, and cash pooling. FX spread is a significant hidden cost in cross-border B2B flows.

Currency management
Reconciliation Systems

Match incoming funds to invoices and purchase orders. Manual reconciliation remains widespread, driving demand for AI-powered matching tools.

II
End-to-End

The Payment Process

From invoice issuance to final reconciliation, a B2B payment passes through eight distinct stages. Total elapsed time ranges from same-day to T+5 depending on the rail and geography.

"The bottleneck is rarely the payment network itself — it is the compliance, approval, and data-matching processes that surround it."

1
Invoice issuance & PO matching

Supplier issues invoice. Buyer's AP system performs three-way match: purchase order, goods receipt, and invoice. Discrepancies trigger manual review and can delay payment by days.

ERP AP automation T+0 to hours
2
Internal approval workflow

Payment routed through spend-limit controls and multi-signatory approval chains. Large payments often require treasury, CFO, or board-level sign-off. Most delays in the overall cycle originate here.

Workflow automation Spend controls Hours to days
3
Compliance & fraud screening

AML checks, sanctions screening against OFAC, EU, and UN lists, KYB verification of the beneficiary, and anomaly-scoring against transaction history. Automated in leading platforms but still manual-heavy at smaller institutions.

AML OFAC / sanctions KYB Minutes to hours
4
FX conversion & liquidity check

If cross-currency, FX rate is locked (spot or pre-hedged forward). Treasury verifies available cash position. Netting centres aggregate offsetting flows to reduce gross settlement volume.

Spot FX Forward hedging Cash netting Real-time if hedged
5
Payment initiation

Payment instruction formatted and transmitted via the chosen rail — SWIFT wire, ACH, RTP, SEPA, or internal book transfer. ISO 20022 MX messages carry structured remittance data alongside the payment.

SWIFT MT/MX ACH RTP / FedNow T+0 to T+1
6
Interbank clearing

Payment obligations netted and exchanged between sending and receiving institutions via clearinghouse (CHIPS, ACH, EBA) or correspondent banking chain. Each correspondent hop adds latency and potential fee deduction.

CHIPS ACH batch Correspondent chain Same/next day
7
Settlement

Final irrevocable transfer of funds across central bank accounts (RTGS). For securities, Delivery-versus-Payment (DvP) ensures simultaneous exchange of assets and cash, eliminating settlement risk.

RTGS DvP T+0 (RTGS) / T+1
8
Reconciliation & reporting

Beneficiary matches received funds to open invoices. Both parties update ERP records. Regulatory reporting (Basel III, EMIR), tax documentation, and audit trail generation complete the cycle.

AR matching ERP sync Audit trail Post-settlement

IV
Mechanics

Cross-Border Flows

International B2B payments are the most complex and costly category. A payment from Singapore to Germany can traverse four banks and two clearinghouses before arriving — each hop adding time, cost, and a compliance check.

The correspondent banking model — built on bilateral nostro/vostro account relationships — has been the backbone of cross-border payments for over a century. It is effective but inefficient: fees accumulate at each correspondent hop, transparency is limited, and settlement can span multiple business days.

V
Strategic View

Pain Points & the Road Ahead

Despite a century of incremental improvement, the B2B payments industry still bears structural inefficiencies. The following represents the landscape of friction versus the forces addressing it.

Persistent pain points
—
Slow cross-border settlement

Capital locked in correspondent chain for 1–5 days. For companies managing tight working capital, this represents a material cost of float.

—
Opaque, compounding fees

Each correspondent hop may deduct fees from principal. Receiving amount is often uncertain at time of initiation — a compliance and accounting problem.

—
Remittance data separation

Legacy MT format truncates invoice references and structured data, forcing manual reconciliation at the beneficiary. Estimated cost: $21B annually.

—
Duplicate compliance overhead

Each institution in a payment chain independently screens the same transaction. There is no shared compliance intelligence across the network.

—
FX spread opacity

Correspondent banks embed margin into exchange rates. Mid-market spreads are rarely disclosed, obscuring the true cost of cross-border transactions.

—
Cheque persistence

45% of US B2B payments still use paper cheques, creating processing delays, fraud exposure, and manual handling costs.

Emerging solutions
+
ISO 20022 global migration

Structured, rich data fields travel with every payment. Enables automated reconciliation, regulatory reporting, and enhanced fraud analytics.

+
Real-time rail expansion

FedNow (US, 2023), PIX (Brazil), UPI (India), and SEPA Instant are enabling 24/7/365 settlement. Adoption in B2B is accelerating past consumer use cases.

+
SWIFT GPI & tracking

Unique end-to-end transaction reference (UETR) enables real-time status tracking across the correspondent chain. 90% of GPI payments settle within 24 hours.

+
Embedded finance & API banking

Payment capabilities integrated directly into ERP, procurement, and supply chain platforms. Removes friction at initiation and reduces manual handoffs.

+
CBDC & tokenised settlement

Wholesale CBDC pilots (mBridge, Project Dunbar) aim to eliminate the correspondent layer entirely — enabling atomic cross-border settlement in seconds.

+
AI-driven reconciliation

Machine learning models match remittances to invoices at scale, reducing DSO and eliminating the largest manual workload in corporate treasury teams.
